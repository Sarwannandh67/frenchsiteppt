The 100 Years' War & Joan of Arc

A pivotal conflict that shaped French national identity and featured one of history's most remarkable heroines

The Conflict That Defined a Nation

The Hundred Years' War (1337-1453) was one of the most significant conflicts of the Middle Ages, a series of intermittent battles between the kingdoms of England and France. What began as a dispute over succession to the French throne evolved into a struggle that would fundamentally reshape both nations and establish a stronger sense of national identity, particularly for France.

Did You Know?

Despite its name, the Hundred Years' War actually lasted 116 years, with several periods of peace interspersed throughout the conflict.

The war began when King Edward III of England claimed the French throne following the death of King Charles IV of France. This claim was based on Edward's maternal lineage, as his mother was Charles IV's sister. The French nobility, however, rejected this claim and instead crowned Philip VI of Valois as king, citing the Salic law that prohibited succession through the female line.

Major Phases of the War

1337-1360

Edwardian Phase

English victories at Crécy (1346) and Poitiers (1356), culminating in the Treaty of Brétigny (1360), which granted England significant territories in France.

1369-1389

Caroline Phase

French recovery under Charles V, who reclaimed many lost territories through strategic warfare rather than pitched battles.

1415-1429

Lancastrian Phase

Renewed English success under Henry V, including the decisive victory at Agincourt (1415), followed by the Treaty of Troyes (1420), which disinherited the French dauphin.

1429-1453

Final Phase

French resurgence inspired by Joan of Arc, leading to Charles VII's coronation and eventual French victory with the Battle of Castillon (1453).

Joan of Arc: The Maid of Orléans

A 19th-century artistic representation of Joan of Arc in armor

In the darkest hour of the French cause, when much of northern France was under English control and the city of Orléans was under siege, a young peasant girl named Joan of Arc (Jeanne d'Arc) emerged as an unlikely savior. Born around 1412 in Domrémy, a small village in northeastern France, Joan claimed to have received visions from Saint Michael, Saint Catherine, and Saint Margaret instructing her to support Charles VII and recover France from English domination.

Joan's Remarkable Journey

At just 17 years old, Joan convinced a local commander to take her to the French court at Chinon, where she eventually gained an audience with the dauphin, Charles VII. After being examined by theologians at Poitiers who found nothing heretical in her claims, Joan was given a small army and sent to relieve the siege of Orléans.

"I am not afraid... I was born to do this." — Joan of Arc

Military Achievements

In May 1429, against all odds, Joan led the French forces to victory at Orléans, lifting the siege in just nine days. This stunning success was followed by a series of victories that cleared the way to Reims, where Charles VII was crowned king on July 17, 1429, with Joan at his side. These victories not only reversed the military situation but also reinvigorated French morale and national identity.

Military Innovation

Joan advocated for more aggressive tactics and greater use of artillery, helping to modernize French military strategy during her brief career.

Capture and Trial

Joan's military career was short-lived. In May 1430, she was captured by Burgundian troops (allies of the English) during a skirmish outside Compiègne. The English purchased her from the Burgundians and put her on trial for heresy and dressing as a man. After a politically motivated trial, Joan was found guilty and burned at the stake in Rouen on May 30, 1431, at approximately 19 years of age.

Legacy and Canonization

Twenty-five years after her execution, a retrial process authorized by Pope Callixtus III examined the original trial, found it deeply flawed, and declared Joan innocent. In 1920, nearly 500 years after her death, Joan was canonized as a saint by the Roman Catholic Church. Today, she remains one of France's greatest national heroines, symbolizing courage, faith, and patriotism.

Historical Significance and Legacy

Military Innovations

The Hundred Years' War saw significant developments in warfare, including the declining effectiveness of heavily armored knights and the rising importance of infantry and longbowmen. The increased use of gunpowder weapons, particularly cannons, revolutionized siege warfare and castle design.

National Identity

Perhaps the most profound impact of the war was the strengthening of national identities in both England and France. For the French in particular, the struggle against English domination fostered a sense of unity and patriotism that transcended feudal loyalties. Joan of Arc became a powerful symbol of this emerging national consciousness.

Artistic depiction of Joan of Arc at the Siege of Orléans

Political Transformation

The war accelerated the transition from feudal monarchies to more centralized nation-states. To finance the war, both kingdoms developed more sophisticated taxation systems and administrative structures. In France, the war ultimately strengthened royal authority, while in England, it contributed to political instability that would later erupt in the Wars of the Roses.

Cultural Impact

Joan of Arc has inspired countless works of art, literature, music, and film across the centuries. From Shakespeare's "Henry VI" to Mark Twain's "Personal Recollections of Joan of Arc," from Verdi's opera "Giovanna d'Arco" to Carl Theodor Dreyer's silent film "The Passion of Joan of Arc," her story continues to captivate the imagination of artists and audiences worldwide.

"Joan of Arc was not stuck at the cross-roads, either by rejecting all the paths like Tolstoy, or by accepting them all like Nietzsche. She chose a path, and went down it like a thunderbolt." — G.K. Chesterton
